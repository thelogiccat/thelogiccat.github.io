# Why Time Feels Faster as We Grow Older

The subjective feeling that time speeds up with age is a well-studied phenomenon in psychology and neuroscience. It is not a change in physical time, but a change in how the brain encodes and reconstructs time.

---
## Proportional Theory (The Math of a Lifetime)➗✖️➕➖

First popularized by philosopher Paul Janet in 1897, the proportional theory suggests that we perceive a period of time relative to the total amount of time we have already lived.

To a 5-year-old child, a single year represents a massive 20% of their entire existence. That year feels incredibly long because it constitutes a huge portion of their total life experience.

However, by the time you reach 50 years old, a single year is just 2% of your life. Because it represents a much smaller fraction of your accumulated existence, your brain subconsciously compresses its significance.

As you age, each year becomes a smaller fraction of your life.

- Age 10 → 1 year = 10% of life  
- Age 20 → 1 year = 5%  
- Age 40 → 1 year = 2.5%

🧠The brain encodes time relatively, making later years feel shorter.

---
## Novelty and Memory Density

Time feels longer when more unique memories are created.

### Childhood = High novelty
- New social groups
- First-time experiences
- Dense memory formation

### Adulthood = Routine Compression
- Repetition increases
- Familiar environment
- Fewer distinct memory markers
- Predictable daily structure

When your brain encounters brand-new information, it processes it deeply and records vivid, highly detailed memories. When you look back at that period, the sheer density of those rich memories makes the time interval feel incredibly stretched out. Driving to work, sitting at a desk, grocery shopping, and watching TV are all processed by the brain as "predictable data." To save energy, the brain stops recording the fine details of repetitive events.

➡️ Fewer unique memories = time feels shorter in hindsight
---

## Attention and Cognitive Automation

As routines become automatic:
- Less attention is required
- Fewer “memory snapshots” are stored

👁️This reduces perceived duration in hindsight.

---
## The Ageing Brain👨‍🦳👩‍🦳

As we age, the neural networks in our brains become larger and more complex, and the pathways that signals must travel grow longer. Simultaneously, the rate at which our brains process visual info slows down.

The Frame Rate Analogy: Think of a child's brain like a high-speed camera capturing 120 frames per second. Because it captures so many images, the "movie" of life plays back in rich, slow-motion detail. An older adult’s brain operates more like an older camera capturing fewer frames per second. Because fewer frames are recorded in the same physical span of time, the action appears to speed up.

## Dopamine and Novelty Response

Dopamine signals novelty and prediction error.

- Childhood → high novelty → strong time segmentation  
- Adulthood → lower novelty → smoother experience flow  

⚡Less segmentation = faster perceived passage of time.

## Routine Compression Effect (Retrospective Time Compression)

A key mechanism is event chunking.

The brain groups repetitive experiences:
Example:

- “Workday 1”
- “Workday 2”
- “Workday 3”

Eventually becomes:

“A normal work week”

So instead of storing 5 distinct days, the brain stores 1 compressed template.

⬇️This significantly reduces perceived elapsed time as we judge time mostly through memory, not real-time perception.

## Modern Acceleration Factors (Digital Era)

Modern life amplifies time compression:

- Key contributors
- Algorithmic scrolling (reduces event boundaries)
- Reduced physical variation in daily life
- Increased repetition of digital environments
- Constant partial attention

This creates:

- fewer encoded “days”✅
- weaker memory boundaries✅
- stronger retrospective compression✅

---
## How To Slow Down Time Again🧘

While you cannot change your age or your biology, you can actively manipulate your perception of time by fighting back against routine. If you want to make your days feel longer and more memorable, try incorporating the following habits into your life:

Seek Out Novelty: Break your routines. Take a different route to work, try a new hobby, travel to unfamiliar places, or cook foods you’ve never tried before.

Practice Mindfulness: Force your brain to anchor itself in the present. Pay active attention to the sensory details around you—the sounds, smells, and textures of your current environment.

Keep Learning: Pick up a musical instrument, learn a new language, or read deeply about unfamiliar subjects. Forcing your brain to build new neural pathways mimics the high-novelty environment of youth.

Time will always move forward at the exact same physical speed, but by filling your life with new experiences and conscious awareness, you can ensure that your memories of it remain vast, rich, and beautifully stretched out.
